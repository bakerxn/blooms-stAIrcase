1. Welcome & What This Is

Generative AI arrived in classrooms faster than most institutions could respond. Faculty were left to figure it out individually, often without the time, the frameworks, or the permission to experiment carefully. We built Bloom's stAIrcase because we believe that gap is solvable — and that cautious, thoughtful faculty are exactly the people whose voices should shape how AI gets integrated into higher education.

This site combines two well-established frameworks — Anderson and Krathwohl's revision of Bloom's Taxonomy and Dr. Kara Kennedy's AI Literacy Framework — into an interactive resource that helps faculty design AI-integrated activities grounded in pedagogical theory rather than trend. Whether you're AI-curious or AI-skeptical, whether you're redesigning one assignment or reimagining a whole course, we've tried to make a space where you can think carefully about what role AI should play in your students' learning.

Bloom's stAIrcase is built and maintained by Brigette Meskell, MLIS and Nicole Baker, MSED, MSLIS. The site is open-access and licensed under Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike 4.0.

2. How to Use This Site

The site has four main areas. Most faculty will find value in exploring them in this order, but they're designed to stand on their own too — jump in wherever makes sense.

  1. Explore the visual diagram The homepage shows Bloom's Taxonomy as an interactive diagram: four Knowledge Dimensions (Factual, Conceptual, Procedural, Metacognitive) crossed with six Cognitive Processes (Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create). Each intersection holds a verb describing what students do at that level — click any cell to see example activities and AI literacy connections. Start exploring.
  2. Browse the AI Literacy Framework Our adapted version of Dr. Kara Kennedy's seven-competency framework for AI literacy — from knowing which tool to use, to understanding legal and ethical implications, to applying AI within a specific professional field. Click any competency to learn what it includes and why it matters. View the framework.
  3. Design an activity Our Activity Designer walks you through a structured form about your course, students, and goals — then generates a comprehensive prompt you can paste into your preferred AI tool. The AI then starts a collaborative conversation to help you design an activity tailored to your specific context. Try the Activity Designer.
  4. Browse and submit activities Once you've designed an activity, consider submitting it so other educators can benefit. The All Activities page collects community submissions organized by discipline and Bloom's classification. See all activities.
Accessibility note: Every interactive element on this site is keyboard-navigable and screen-reader-accessible. If you prefer a non-visual alternative to the main diagram, we've built an accessible table view that contains the same content in a screen-reader-optimized format.

3. Using Bloom's Taxonomy for AI Activity Design

If AI is already changing what's possible — and what's at stake — in student learning, then we need better ways to think about where and how AI should show up in our courses. Bloom's Taxonomy, especially as revised by Anderson and Krathwohl in 2001, gives us a map for that thinking.

The Core Idea

Generative AI is good at some cognitive work and not at others. It excels at tasks involving Factual and Conceptual knowledge at lower cognitive levels — remembering, summarizing, and rephrasing. It struggles with genuine Evaluation and Creation, especially when those require contextual judgment, ethical reasoning, or synthesis across a student's own lived experience. It is essentially incapable of Metacognitive work — knowing what you don't know, reflecting on your own learning strategies, or monitoring your own understanding.

This matters pedagogically because an AI-integrated activity designed thoughtfully uses AI to support lower-order work (so students can spend their effort on higher-order thinking), while protecting the higher-order and metacognitive work that only humans can genuinely do. An AI-integrated activity designed carelessly lets AI do the higher-order thinking for students — which is exactly what faculty worry about, and rightly so.

A Practical Approach

When designing an activity with AI integration, we suggest working through these questions:

  1. What do you want students to learn? Identify which Knowledge Dimension (Factual, Conceptual, Procedural, or Metacognitive) the activity primarily targets.
  2. What cognitive work should they do? Identify which Cognitive Process level (Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, or Create) the activity primarily targets.
  3. Where does AI genuinely help? Consider where AI could reduce busywork, scaffold weaker skills, or provide feedback — without taking over the learning itself.
  4. What must stay human? Name the specific thinking, decision-making, or reflection that students must do without AI assistance. In our Activity Designer, this is called Protected Student Work.
  5. How will you know students did it? Design assessment that captures the human cognitive work, not just the final artifact.

A Note on Universal Design for Learning

Throughout our work, we draw on Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles: offering multiple means of engagement, representation, and action/expression. UDL and AI integration work well together — both assume students come in with different strengths, contexts, and needs, and both push us toward pedagogical flexibility rather than one-size-fits-all instruction. When using our Activity Designer, UDL principles are baked into every generated prompt, shaping the AI's recommendations throughout.

4. Our AI Statement

We built this site about thoughtful AI integration. It would be dishonest — and pedagogically incoherent — not to be transparent about how we used AI in building it ourselves. The principles we teach for faculty designing AI-integrated activities are the same principles we used to make this site.

How AI Was Used

We used generative AI as a collaborator throughout the process of building Bloom's stAIrcase. Specifically, AI assisted with writing and debugging code, drafting prose we then edited, suggesting design alternatives, organizing complex information into the structures you see, and catching errors we wouldn't have noticed on our own.

What Stayed Human

The pedagogical decisions — which frameworks to adopt, how to adapt them, which competencies to emphasize, how to define "protected student work," what a responsible AI-integrated activity looks like — are ours. We vetted every source cited here, read Kennedy's framework and Heer's adaptation of Anderson and Krathwohl in their original contexts, and made editorial choices about what to include, exclude, and emphasize. When AI suggested something pedagogically unsound, we pushed back or discarded it. When AI caught a framing we hadn't considered, we took it seriously but checked its reasoning against our own expertise and the published literature.

Our Stance

We are neither AI boosters nor AI refusers. We believe generative AI is already part of higher education — the question isn't whether to engage with it but how to engage with it thoughtfully, ethically, and with our students' learning at the center. We also believe faculty have been asked to figure this out largely alone, which is unfair. This site is one small contribution toward making the thinking more collective.

What We Recommend for Faculty

If you're using AI in your teaching — whether to design activities, give feedback on student work, or build course materials — we recommend applying the same principle we've tried to model here: be transparent about what AI did, name what you kept human, and be explicit with your students about both. The pedagogical integrity of AI-integrated work depends on that honesty.

A Note on Your Data

We don't track visitors, set advertising cookies, or collect personal information about how you use this site. The only data we store on your device is a small browser preference flag — so we don't show you the welcome message every time you visit. We never see this flag; it lives only in your browser.

The only personal information we ever collect is what you choose to share with us when you submit an activity (such as your name, email, and institution). We use that information solely to follow up about your submission and never share or sell it.
