What is Metacognitive Knowledge?

Metacognitive knowledge is knowledge of cognition in general as well as awareness and knowledge of one's own cognition. It represents the most abstract form of knowledge in the Knowledge Dimension. This type of knowledge involves thinking about thinking—understanding how we learn, what strategies work best in different contexts, and awareness of our own strengths, weaknesses, and cognitive processes.

Unlike the other three knowledge types, metacognitive knowledge is unique because it applies across all disciplines and learning contexts. While factual, conceptual, and procedural knowledge are typically domain-specific, metacognitive knowledge helps students become self-directed learners who can monitor their own understanding, select appropriate strategies, and regulate their learning processes regardless of the subject matter.

Metacognitive knowledge is considered the most abstract because it requires students to step back from the content they're learning and reflect on their own cognitive processes. It's knowledge about knowledge—understanding not just what you know, but how you know it, how you learn best, and how to approach new learning situations strategically.

AI Literacy Connection

AI tools can support metacognitive development by providing feedback, suggesting learning strategies, and helping students reflect on their learning. However, AI can also induce metacognitive laziness if students rely on it without thinking about their own learning processes.

Teaching AI literacy through metacognition might look like, students exercising discernment of when to use AI, when not to, when to reflect on use, and why; students recognizing that AI can't develop their metacognitive knowledge for them; using AI to provide personalized feedback that prompts metacognitive reflection; students evaluating whether AI-suggested strategies are appropriate for them personally; emphasizing that metacognitive knowledge helps students use AI tools more effectively and critically; providing students with opportunities toreflect on how AI use affects their learning and understanding through verbal or writing exercises; developing students' awareness of when AI assistance supports vs. hinders their learning.
