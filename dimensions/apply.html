What is Apply?

Apply involves carrying out or using a procedure in a given situation. When students apply, they use their knowledge to solve problems, complete tasks, or address situations they haven't encountered in exactly the same form before. Application requires both understanding the procedure and recognizing when and how to use it appropriately.

In the revised Bloom's Taxonomy, Apply represents a crucial bridge between lower-order and higher-order thinking. While Remember and Understand focus on acquiring and making sense of knowledge, Apply requires students to actually use that knowledge in practical contexts. This is where learning becomes functional—students demonstrate they can do something with what they know, not just recall or explain it.

Apply sits at a pivotal position in the cognitive process spectrum. It requires more than understanding (you must actually execute procedures), but it's less complex than analyzing, evaluating, or creating (which require breaking down, judging, or generating new ideas). Successful application demonstrates that students have moved beyond passive knowledge to active competence. It's the foundation for problem-solving and real-world use of learning.

Common Misconception

Myth: If students can solve practice problems that are similar to examples, they can apply their knowledge.

Reality: True application involves using procedures in new situations, not just repeating practiced examples. Students who can only solve problems identical to those they've practiced are still at the Remember level (recalling procedures). Application requires recognizing which procedure to use and adapting it to novel contexts. The key indicator of application is transfer to situations that differ from instruction.

Cognitive Processes Within Apply

The Apply category includes two specific cognitive processes that represent different aspects of using procedures.

Executing

Alternative name: Carrying out

Definition: Applying a procedure to a familiar task. Executing occurs when students carry out a procedure in a situation where they've been trained to use that specific procedure. The task is familiar, and students know which procedure to use—they just need to carry it out correctly.

Key characteristics:

  • The procedure to use is clear or specified
  • The task type is familiar from instruction
  • Focus is on accurate execution of known procedures
  • Requires procedural knowledge and careful attention to steps

Example prompts: "Solve this equation...", "Calculate the area...", "Conduct the experiment using...", "Follow the procedure to..."

Implementing

Alternative name: Using

Definition: Applying a procedure to an unfamiliar task. Implementing occurs when students must select and use a procedure in a new situation. The task is unfamiliar, and students must recognize which procedure applies and how to adapt it to the new context. This is true application.

Key characteristics:

  • The task is new or unfamiliar
  • Students must select the appropriate procedure
  • May require modifying or adapting procedures
  • Requires both procedural and conceptual knowledge
  • Involves transfer of learning to new contexts

Example prompts: "Use what you know to solve...", "Apply the concept to...", "How would you approach...", "Determine the best method for..."

Relationship Between Executing and Implementing

Both processes involve using procedures, but they differ in the level of novelty and decision-making required. Executing is more routine—students know what to do and just need to do it correctly. Implementing requires judgment—students must figure out what to do and how to adapt their knowledge to new situations. In educational practice, students typically learn to execute procedures first, then progress to implementing them in novel contexts.

Progression example:

  • Executing: Solve 10 quadratic equations using the quadratic formula (all clearly require the formula)
  • Implementing: Solve various equations and word problems, determining which method is most appropriate for each

Apply Across Disciplines

Science

Executing:

  • Follow the steps of a lab procedure you've been taught
  • Use the formula F=ma to calculate force in standard problems
  • Conduct a titration using the demonstrated technique

Implementing:

  • Design an experiment to test a new hypothesis using scientific method principles
  • Determine which formula to use to solve an unfamiliar physics problem
  • Apply knowledge of chemical reactions to predict products in a new scenario
Mathematics

Executing:

  • Solve equations using the taught algorithm
  • Calculate area using the formula A = πr²
  • Graph linear equations following the demonstrated steps

Implementing:

  • Solve word problems requiring selection of appropriate operations
  • Determine which geometric formula applies to a real-world measurement problem
  • Apply mathematical concepts to model a real-world situation
History

Executing:

  • Use the taught method to analyze a primary source
  • Follow the demonstrated process to create a timeline
  • Apply the citation format you've been taught

Implementing:

  • Apply historical analysis methods to interpret a new document
  • Use understanding of historical patterns to analyze a current event
  • Apply concepts of causation to explain an unfamiliar historical development
Literature & Language Arts

Executing:

  • Use the writing process steps you've been taught
  • Apply grammar rules to correct sentences
  • Follow the demonstrated format for literary analysis

Implementing:

  • Apply rhetorical strategies to write for a new audience and purpose
  • Use literary analysis techniques on an unfamiliar text
  • Apply understanding of narrative structure to craft an original story
Computer Science

Executing:

  • Write code following the syntax you've been taught
  • Use a specific algorithm to sort data
  • Debug code using the demonstrated process

Implementing:

  • Select and apply appropriate data structures for a new problem
  • Use programming concepts to solve an unfamiliar computational challenge
  • Apply debugging strategies to identify and fix errors in novel code

How Students Demonstrate Apply

Students demonstrate application by successfully using procedures to complete tasks and solve problems. The key indicator is whether students can transfer their learning to situations that differ from instruction—not just repeat practiced examples.

Executing Procedures Correctly

Students demonstrate executing when they:

  • Carry out procedures accurately and efficiently
  • Follow steps in the correct sequence
  • Use tools and techniques appropriately
  • Complete familiar tasks successfully
  • Show fluency with practiced procedures

Implementing in New Contexts

Students demonstrate implementing when they:

  • Recognize which procedure applies to a new situation
  • Adapt procedures to fit novel contexts
  • Solve problems that differ from practiced examples
  • Transfer learning across contexts
  • Select appropriate methods from multiple options

Problem-Solving Behaviors

Students showing strong application skills:

  • Approach problems systematically
  • Select appropriate strategies based on problem characteristics
  • Modify approaches when initial attempts don't work
  • Explain why they chose particular procedures
  • Check their work and verify solutions
  • Apply learning in practical, real-world contexts

Observable Behaviors

Students with strong application abilities:

  • Work independently on tasks requiring procedure use
  • Ask strategic questions about when to use different methods
  • Recognize similarities between new problems and familiar ones
  • Adjust procedures appropriately for different contexts
  • Persist when facing unfamiliar applications
  • Explain their problem-solving process clearly
Assessment Tip

To assess application, present students with problems or tasks that are similar to, but not identical to, those practiced during instruction. True application is demonstrated when students can transfer learning to new contexts. Include problems that require selecting among procedures, adapting methods, or applying learning in practical situations. Avoid assessment that only requires students to repeat practiced examples—that assesses recall, not application.

Tips for Educators

Developing Application Skills

  • Start with executing, progress to implementing: Students need to master procedures before they can apply them flexibly in new contexts.
  • Provide varied practice: Give students opportunities to use procedures in diverse situations, not just repeated similar problems.
  • Make connections explicit: Help students see how procedures connect to concepts and when to use them.
  • Use authentic tasks: Provide real-world applications that show why procedures matter.
  • Teach strategic knowledge: Explicitly teach when and why to use different procedures.
  • Scaffold transfer: Gradually increase the novelty of application tasks as students gain competence.
  • Emphasize understanding, not just execution: Students who understand why procedures work can apply them more flexibly.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Only practicing identical problems: If all practice problems are the same, students aren't truly applying—they're just repeating.
  • Assuming understanding equals application: Students can understand concepts without being able to apply them effectively.
  • Not teaching when to use procedures: Knowing how to do something is incomplete without knowing when to do it.
  • Moving to application too quickly: Students need solid understanding before they can apply successfully.
  • Neglecting metacognition: Help students think about their problem-solving process, not just get answers.
  • Providing too much scaffolding: Students need opportunities to struggle productively with application.
AI Literacy Connection

How AI Relates to Application: AI can execute procedures automatically and sometimes implement solutions to novel problems. This changes what application skills students need—the focus shifts from routine execution to strategic selection, adaptation, and verification of procedures.

Teaching Implications:

  • Focus on teaching when and why to use procedures, not just how (AI can handle the "how")
  • Emphasize application tasks that require judgment, creativity, and adaptation
  • Teach students to verify AI-generated solutions and procedures
  • Help students recognize when AI application is appropriate vs. when human judgment is needed
  • Use AI to provide additional practice opportunities with immediate feedback
  • Teach students to evaluate whether AI-suggested approaches are appropriate for specific contexts
  • Emphasize that understanding enables students to apply knowledge more effectively than AI in novel situations

Key Message: While AI can execute many procedures automatically, human application involves strategic thinking, contextual judgment, and creative adaptation that AI cannot replicate. Students need application skills to use AI tools effectively, verify AI outputs, and solve problems that require human insight and judgment. Strong application abilities enable students to be AI users, not just AI consumers.

Practical Strategy

The "Three-Problem Progression" Technique: For any procedure, provide three types of problems: (1) A worked example showing the procedure, (2) Similar problems for executing practice, and (3) Novel problems requiring implementing. This progression helps students move from understanding procedures to applying them flexibly. Always include the third type—that's where true application happens.

Building from Application to Higher-Order Thinking

Application serves as the foundation for more complex cognitive processes:

  • Apply → Analyze: Students must apply procedures to generate information they can then analyze
  • Apply → Evaluate: Application provides the basis for judging effectiveness and making improvements
  • Apply → Create: Students combine and adapt applied procedures to generate new solutions

Ensure students can apply their knowledge effectively before expecting them to analyze, evaluate, or create. At the same time, use application as a stepping stone to higher-order thinking, not an endpoint. The goal is students who can not only use procedures but also think critically about them and innovate beyond them.
