What is Analyze?

Analyze involves breaking material into its constituent parts and determining how the parts relate to one another and to an overall structure or purpose. When students analyze, they examine information systematically, identify components, recognize patterns and relationships, and understand organizational principles. Analysis is essential for deep understanding and critical thinking.

In the revised Bloom's Taxonomy, Analyze represents the first of the higher-order thinking skills. While Remember, Understand, and Apply focus on acquiring and using knowledge, Analyze requires students to think critically about information—to take it apart, examine it, and understand how it works. This cognitive process is fundamental to scholarly inquiry, research, and problem-solving across all disciplines.

Analyze sits at a crucial position in the cognitive spectrum. It builds on understanding (you must comprehend something before you can analyze it) and enables evaluation and creation (you must analyze before you can judge or synthesize). Analysis involves both divergent thinking (breaking things down into parts) and convergent thinking (understanding how parts relate). It's the foundation of critical thinking and academic work.

Common Misconception

Myth: Analysis is just breaking things into parts or listing components.

Reality: True analysis goes beyond identification to examine relationships, patterns, and organizational principles. Students must not only identify parts but also explain how they relate to each other and to the whole, recognize underlying structures, and understand the function of each component. Simply listing parts without examining relationships is closer to Remember or Understand, not Analyze.

Cognitive Processes Within Analyze

The Analyze category includes three specific cognitive processes that represent different aspects of breaking down and examining material.

Differentiating

Alternative names: Discriminating, Distinguishing, Focusing, Selecting

Definition: Distinguishing relevant from irrelevant parts or important from unimportant parts of presented material. Differentiating involves determining which pieces of information are most significant, separating essential from non-essential elements, and focusing on what matters most.

Key characteristics:

  • Identifying which information is relevant to a purpose
  • Separating important from trivial details
  • Distinguishing fact from opinion
  • Recognizing bias or point of view
  • Filtering signal from noise

Example prompts: "Which information is most relevant to...?", "Distinguish between...", "Identify the key factors...", "What evidence supports...?"

Organizing

Alternative names: Finding coherence, Integrating, Outlining, Parsing, Structuring

Definition: Determining how elements fit or function within a structure. Organizing involves identifying patterns, recognizing organizational principles, and understanding how parts work together as a coherent whole. This process reveals the underlying structure of material.

Key characteristics:

  • Identifying patterns and relationships
  • Recognizing organizational structures
  • Creating outlines or diagrams showing relationships
  • Understanding how parts fit together
  • Revealing underlying frameworks

Example prompts: "Outline the structure of...", "How are these elements organized?", "Create a diagram showing...", "Identify the pattern in..."

Attributing

Alternative name: Deconstructing

Definition: Determining the point of view, bias, values, or intent underlying presented material. Attributing involves uncovering the purpose, perspective, or assumptions behind information. This critical process helps students understand not just what is being communicated, but why and from what perspective.

Key characteristics:

  • Identifying author's purpose or intent
  • Recognizing bias or perspective
  • Uncovering underlying assumptions
  • Determining values or ideology
  • Understanding context and influences

Example prompts: "What is the author's purpose?", "Identify the bias in...", "What assumptions underlie...?", "Deconstruct the argument to reveal..."

Relationship Among the Processes

These three processes work together in comprehensive analysis. Differentiating helps identify what's important, organizing reveals how parts relate and fit together, and attributing uncovers the underlying perspective or purpose. Effective analysis often involves all three processes, though specific tasks may emphasize one over others.

Analyze Across Disciplines

Science

Differentiating:

  • Distinguish between dependent and independent variables in an experiment
  • Identify relevant data from a complex dataset
  • Separate correlation from causation in research findings

Organizing:

  • Diagram the relationships in a food web
  • Outline the steps and relationships in a biochemical pathway
  • Map the structure of the periodic table and identify patterns

Attributing:

  • Identify potential sources of bias in a scientific study
  • Determine the assumptions underlying a scientific model
  • Recognize how funding sources might influence research conclusions
Mathematics

Differentiating:

  • Identify which information in a word problem is relevant
  • Distinguish between necessary and sufficient conditions in proofs
  • Separate valid from invalid mathematical reasoning

Organizing:

  • Outline the logical structure of a mathematical proof
  • Identify patterns in numerical sequences or geometric figures
  • Map the relationships among mathematical concepts

Attributing:

  • Identify the assumptions in a mathematical model
  • Determine the purpose of each step in a solution method
  • Recognize the underlying principles that make an algorithm work
History

Differentiating:

  • Distinguish primary from secondary sources
  • Identify which factors were most significant in causing an event
  • Separate fact from interpretation in historical accounts

Organizing:

  • Outline the causes and effects of a historical event
  • Identify patterns in historical developments
  • Map the relationships among historical factors (political, economic, social)

Attributing:

  • Identify the perspective or bias in a historical document
  • Determine the author's purpose in a primary source
  • Recognize how the historian's context influences their interpretation
Literature & Language Arts

Differentiating:

  • Distinguish between major and minor themes in a text
  • Identify which textual evidence best supports an interpretation
  • Separate literal from figurative language

Organizing:

  • Outline the plot structure and identify how events relate
  • Map character relationships and their development
  • Identify patterns in symbolism or imagery

Attributing:

  • Identify the author's purpose or message
  • Determine the narrator's point of view and reliability
  • Recognize how the author's background influences the text
Computer Science

Differentiating:

  • Identify which parts of code are causing an error
  • Distinguish between efficient and inefficient algorithms
  • Separate interface from implementation in program design

Organizing:

  • Diagram the flow of a program or algorithm
  • Outline the structure of a database and identify relationships
  • Map the architecture of a software system

Attributing:

  • Identify the purpose of each function or module
  • Determine the design principles underlying a code structure
  • Recognize assumptions in algorithm design

How Students Demonstrate Analyze

Students demonstrate analytical thinking by breaking down information, examining relationships, and uncovering underlying structures or purposes. Analysis is shown through systematic examination, not just description or summary.

Breaking Down and Examining Components

Students with analytical skills can:

  • Identify the parts or elements of something
  • Explain the function or role of each component
  • Distinguish important from unimportant elements
  • Separate different types of information (fact vs. opinion, cause vs. effect)
  • Focus on relevant details while filtering out noise

Identifying Relationships and Patterns

Students demonstrate analysis by:

  • Explaining how parts relate to each other
  • Recognizing patterns and organizational structures
  • Creating visual representations (diagrams, outlines, maps) showing relationships
  • Identifying cause-and-effect relationships
  • Understanding how parts contribute to the whole

Uncovering Purpose and Perspective

Students show analytical thinking when they:

  • Identify bias, assumptions, or point of view
  • Determine the purpose or intent behind information
  • Recognize how context influences meaning
  • Question underlying premises
  • Understand multiple perspectives on an issue

Observable Behaviors

Students with strong analytical abilities:

  • Ask probing questions that go beyond surface understanding
  • Look for evidence to support or refute claims
  • Identify inconsistencies or contradictions
  • Make connections between different pieces of information
  • Question assumptions and challenge ideas constructively
  • Use analytical frameworks or tools systematically
  • Support their analysis with specific evidence
Assessment Tip

To assess analysis, ask students to break down complex material, identify relationships, or uncover underlying structures. Good analytical tasks require students to go beyond description to examine how and why. Use prompts like: "Analyze the relationship between...", "What patterns do you see in...?", "Identify the assumptions underlying...", "How do the parts contribute to...?" Look for evidence of systematic examination, not just identification of parts.

Tips for Educators

Developing Analytical Skills

  • Model analytical thinking: Think aloud as you analyze material, showing students how to break things down and examine relationships.
  • Teach analytical frameworks: Provide students with tools and structures for analysis (graphic organizers, analytical questions, frameworks).
  • Start with guided analysis: Begin with structured analytical tasks before expecting independent analysis.
  • Ask analytical questions: Regularly pose questions that require analysis: "How does X relate to Y?", "What patterns do you notice?", "What's the author's purpose?"
  • Provide complex material: Analysis requires material rich enough to be broken down and examined.
  • Encourage evidence-based reasoning: Require students to support their analytical claims with specific evidence.
  • Use visual tools: Diagrams, concept maps, and outlines help students see relationships and structures.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Confusing description with analysis: Students who only describe or summarize aren't analyzing. Push them to examine relationships and structures.
  • Accepting unsupported claims: Analysis requires evidence. Don't accept assertions without supporting details.
  • Focusing only on parts: Analysis involves both breaking down AND understanding how parts relate to the whole.
  • Not providing enough complexity: Simple material doesn't require or support analysis.
  • Skipping foundational understanding: Students can't analyze what they don't understand. Ensure comprehension first.
  • Not teaching analytical vocabulary: Students need language for analysis (e.g., "relationship," "pattern," "assumption," "bias").
AI Literacy Connection

How AI Relates to Analysis: AI can perform certain types of analysis (pattern recognition, data analysis, text analysis), but it lacks the critical judgment, contextual understanding, and creative insight that characterize human analytical thinking. AI analysis is often superficial or misses nuance.

Teaching Implications:

  • Use AI to generate initial analyses that students then critique and improve
  • Have students analyze AI outputs to identify what the AI missed or got wrong
  • Teach students that AI can identify patterns but may not understand their significance
  • Emphasize analytical skills that AI cannot replicate: contextual judgment, recognizing bias, understanding purpose
  • Use AI to help organize information, then have students perform deeper analysis
  • Teach students to analyze AI-generated content critically, not accept it at face value
  • Help students understand that human analysis considers context, ethics, and implications that AI misses

Key Message: Analytical skills are more important than ever in the age of AI. While AI can process and pattern-match, humans must analyze critically—questioning assumptions, recognizing bias, understanding context, and making nuanced judgments. Students with strong analytical skills can evaluate AI outputs, identify their limitations, and provide the critical thinking that AI lacks.

Practical Strategy

The "Three-Level Analysis" Technique: Teach students to analyze at three levels: (1) Identify the parts (what are the components?), (2) Examine relationships (how do parts relate?), and (3) Uncover purpose/perspective (why is it structured this way? what's the intent?). This framework ensures comprehensive analysis beyond simple identification.

Building from Analysis to Higher-Order Thinking

Analysis enables the highest levels of thinking:

  • Analyze → Evaluate: You must analyze before you can make informed judgments
  • Analyze → Create: Understanding how things work (analysis) enables you to create new things

Analysis is the foundation of critical thinking, research, and scholarly work. Students who can analyze effectively can think independently, question assumptions, and engage deeply with complex material. These skills are essential for academic success and informed citizenship.
