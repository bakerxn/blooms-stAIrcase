What is Understand?

Understand involves constructing meaning from instructional messages, including oral, written, and graphic communication. When students understand, they can explain ideas or concepts in their own words, provide examples, make connections, and see relationships. Understanding goes beyond mere recall to involve making sense of information and integrating it with existing knowledge.

In the revised Bloom's Taxonomy, Understand (called "Comprehension" in the original taxonomy) represents the first level of genuine cognitive engagement with material. While Remember involves retrieving information as learned, Understand requires students to transform information into a form that makes sense to them. This transformation is evidence that students have constructed personal meaning from the material.

Understanding is foundational for all higher-order thinking. Students cannot effectively apply, analyze, evaluate, or create with knowledge they don't understand. However, understanding alone is not sufficient for expertise—students must also be able to use their understanding in practical and creative ways. Understand sits at the lower-order end of the cognitive spectrum, but it represents a crucial bridge between simple recall and more complex cognitive processes.

Common Misconception

Myth: If students can repeat information back, they understand it.

Reality: Students can memorize and repeat information without truly understanding it. True understanding is demonstrated when students can explain concepts in their own words, provide new examples, make connections to other ideas, and apply their knowledge in different contexts. Teachers must look beyond simple recall to assess genuine understanding.

Cognitive Processes Within Understand

The Understand category includes seven specific cognitive processes that represent different ways students construct meaning from information.

Interpreting

Alternative names: Clarifying, Paraphrasing, Representing, Translating

Definition: Converting information from one form of representation to another. This includes changing words into other words (paraphrasing), converting words to pictures or diagrams, or translating from one language to another.

Example: Paraphrasing a passage in your own words; creating a graph from numerical data; explaining a diagram in words

Exemplifying

Alternative names: Illustrating, Instantiating

Definition: Finding a specific example or illustration of a concept or principle. This involves identifying instances that fit a general category or providing concrete examples of abstract ideas.

Example: Giving an example of a metaphor; identifying instances of democracy; providing examples of renewable energy

Classifying

Alternative names: Categorizing, Subsuming

Definition: Determining that something belongs to a category. This involves recognizing the features of examples and matching them to known categories or concepts.

Example: Classifying animals by their characteristics; categorizing numbers as prime or composite; identifying types of governments

Summarizing

Alternative names: Abstracting, Generalizing

Definition: Abstracting a general theme or major points from information. This involves identifying the most important information and presenting it in condensed form.

Example: Writing a summary of a chapter; identifying the main idea of a passage; creating an abstract of a research article

Inferring

Alternative names: Concluding, Extrapolating, Interpolating, Predicting

Definition: Drawing a logical conclusion from presented information. This involves going beyond explicit information to identify patterns, make predictions, or draw conclusions.

Example: Predicting what will happen next in a story; inferring the author's purpose; drawing conclusions from data

Comparing

Alternative names: Contrasting, Mapping, Matching

Definition: Detecting correspondences between two ideas, objects, or situations. This involves identifying similarities and differences between things.

Example: Comparing two historical events; contrasting different theories; mapping relationships between concepts

Explaining

Alternative names: Constructing models

Definition: Constructing a cause-and-effect model of a system. This involves describing how something works or why something happens, often by identifying the relationships among parts.

Example: Explaining how photosynthesis works; describing why the seasons change; explaining the causes of a historical event

Understand Across Disciplines

Science

Interpreting: Translate a food web diagram into a written description; paraphrase the steps of mitosis

Exemplifying: Provide examples of chemical changes; identify instances of Newton's third law in everyday life

Classifying: Categorize organisms by their characteristics; classify rocks as igneous, sedimentary, or metamorphic

Summarizing: Summarize the main findings of an experiment; identify the key points of a scientific article

Inferring: Predict the products of a chemical reaction; infer the function of an organism's structure

Comparing: Compare plant and animal cells; contrast renewable and nonrenewable resources

Explaining: Explain how the water cycle works; describe why objects fall at the same rate regardless of mass

Mathematics

Interpreting: Represent a word problem as an equation; translate a graph into a verbal description

Exemplifying: Give examples of rational numbers; provide instances where the distributive property applies

Classifying: Classify triangles by their angles; categorize functions as linear or nonlinear

Summarizing: State the main idea of a proof; summarize the steps to solve a type of problem

Inferring: Predict the next term in a sequence; infer the rule governing a pattern

Comparing: Compare different methods for solving equations; contrast area and perimeter

Explaining: Explain why the Pythagorean theorem works; describe how compound interest grows over time

History

Interpreting: Paraphrase a primary source document; translate a political cartoon's message into words

Exemplifying: Provide examples of imperialism; identify instances of civil disobedience in history

Classifying: Categorize governments as democratic or authoritarian; classify wars by their causes

Summarizing: Summarize the main causes of World War I; identify the key outcomes of the Civil Rights Movement

Inferring: Infer the author's bias from a historical account; predict consequences of a historical decision

Comparing: Compare the American and French Revolutions; contrast different historical interpretations

Explaining: Explain why the Industrial Revolution began in England; describe how the Constitution balances power

Literature & Language Arts

Interpreting: Paraphrase a poem; translate figurative language into literal meaning

Exemplifying: Provide examples of symbolism in a text; identify instances of irony

Classifying: Classify texts by genre; categorize characters by their traits

Summarizing: Summarize the plot of a story; identify the main argument of an essay

Inferring: Infer a character's motivation; predict how a story will end based on foreshadowing

Comparing: Compare two characters; contrast different authors' styles

Explaining: Explain how the setting affects the mood; describe why the author chose a particular narrative structure

Computer Science

Interpreting: Explain what a code snippet does in plain language; translate pseudocode into actual code

Exemplifying: Provide examples of loops in real programs; identify instances where recursion is useful

Classifying: Classify data structures by their properties; categorize algorithms by their efficiency

Summarizing: Summarize what a function does; identify the main purpose of a program

Inferring: Predict the output of code; infer the purpose of a variable

Comparing: Compare different sorting algorithms; contrast object-oriented and functional programming

Explaining: Explain how a search algorithm works; describe why certain data structures are more efficient

How Students Demonstrate Understanding

Students demonstrate understanding by showing they can construct meaning from information. Unlike Remember, which involves retrieving information as learned, Understand requires students to transform, connect, and make sense of information in ways that show genuine comprehension.

Explaining in Their Own Words

Students with understanding can:

  • Paraphrase information without losing meaning
  • Explain concepts using different words than the original source
  • Describe ideas clearly to someone unfamiliar with the topic
  • Translate between different forms of representation (words to diagrams, etc.)

Providing Examples and Non-Examples

Understanding is shown when students:

  • Generate their own examples of concepts
  • Identify instances that fit or don't fit categories
  • Recognize concepts in new contexts
  • Explain why something is or isn't an example

Making Connections

Students demonstrate understanding by:

  • Relating new information to prior knowledge
  • Identifying similarities and differences
  • Seeing patterns and relationships
  • Connecting ideas across topics or disciplines

Observable Behaviors

Students with strong understanding:

  • Ask questions that show they're making sense of material
  • Can explain "why" and "how," not just "what"
  • Recognize when something doesn't make sense and seek clarification
  • Can summarize main ideas accurately
  • Make predictions based on their understanding
  • Apply their understanding to interpret new information
  • Explain concepts using analogies and metaphors
Assessment Tip

To assess understanding, ask students to explain in their own words, provide new examples, compare and contrast, summarize, or predict. Avoid questions that can be answered by simple recall or recognition. Look for evidence that students have constructed personal meaning, not just memorized information. Good understanding questions include: "Explain why...", "Give an example of...", "How is X similar to Y?", "What would happen if...?"

Tips for Educators

Promoting Deep Understanding

  • Check for understanding frequently: Don't assume students understand just because they're quiet or nodding. Ask them to explain, provide examples, or make connections.
  • Use multiple representations: Present information in various forms (words, diagrams, examples, analogies) to help students construct robust understanding.
  • Encourage elaboration: Have students explain ideas in their own words and connect new information to what they already know.
  • Provide examples and non-examples: Help students understand concepts by showing what they are and what they aren't.
  • Make thinking visible: Model your own understanding processes by thinking aloud as you interpret, compare, or explain.
  • Use graphic organizers: Tools like concept maps, Venn diagrams, and comparison charts help students organize and connect information.
  • Ask "why" and "how" questions: Push students beyond surface-level recall to deeper understanding.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Confusing recall with understanding: Students who can repeat information may not understand it. Always check for genuine comprehension.
  • Moving too quickly: Understanding takes time. Allow students to process, discuss, and make connections.
  • Providing only one representation: Students need multiple ways of encountering information to build understanding.
  • Not addressing misconceptions: Check for and correct misunderstandings before they become entrenched.
  • Focusing only on correct answers: Understanding is revealed in how students explain their thinking, not just whether they get the right answer.
AI Literacy Connection

How AI Relates to Understanding: AI can provide explanations, examples, and multiple representations of concepts. However, AI cannot verify whether students truly understand—it can only check if their responses match expected patterns. True understanding requires human judgment and interaction.

Teaching Implications:

  • Use AI to generate multiple explanations and examples to support understanding
  • Have students explain concepts to AI and evaluate the AI's responses for accuracy
  • Teach students that AI explanations may be technically correct but not aid understanding
  • Help students recognize that understanding requires personal meaning-making, not just reading AI explanations
  • Use AI to provide immediate feedback on students' explanations and examples
  • Emphasize that students must verify their understanding, not just accept AI-generated content
  • Teach students to ask AI for different representations when they don't understand

Key Message: AI can support understanding by providing explanations and examples, but it cannot replace the cognitive work of constructing meaning. Students must actively engage with information—whether from AI or other sources—to build genuine understanding. Understanding enables students to evaluate AI explanations critically and recognize when AI outputs are incorrect or misleading.

Practical Strategy

The "Teach It Back" Technique: Have students explain concepts to a partner as if teaching them for the first time. This requires students to construct their own understanding and identify gaps in their comprehension. Students who can teach a concept clearly demonstrate genuine understanding, while those who struggle reveal areas needing further instruction.

Building from Understanding to Higher-Order Thinking

Understanding is the foundation for more complex cognitive processes:

  • Understand → Apply: Students must understand concepts before they can apply them to new situations
  • Understand → Analyze: Analysis requires understanding what you're breaking down and examining
  • Understand → Evaluate: You can't make sound judgments about things you don't understand
  • Understand → Create: Creation requires understanding the elements you're working with

Ensure students have solid understanding before pushing them to higher-order thinking. At the same time, don't stop at understanding—use it as a springboard for application, analysis, evaluation, and creation.
